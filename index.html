J.R.R Tolkien Father of Fantasy

J.R.R Tolkien, the man dubbed 'Father of Fantasy', influenced the world in many ways through his life and works. He was a noted philologist and professor, in addition to being the author of beloved fantasy works including The Lord of the Rings and The Hobbit. Perhaps one of the most remarkable aspects of his creative work is its intense attention to detail. He created languages, detailed maps, and genealogical tables for his characters, seeding the ground for a rich fan base that has thrived over nearly a century. His work continues to inspire games, movies, and writing in the genre of high fantasy.

Main Works

The Hobbit

Published Date:

21 September 1937


Bilbo Baggins is a hobbit who lives a quiet life, until it is upset by a visit from a wizard named Gandalf. He wants Bilbo to help a group of dwarves take back the Mountain from Smaug, a dragon. Bilbo is unsure he wants to help, but he is drawn into the adventure by Gandalf.

The Fellowship of The Ring

Published Date:

29 July 1954


Young Frodo Baggins finds himself faced with an immense task, as his elderly cousin Bilbo entrusts the One Ring to his care. Frodo must leave his home and make a perilous journey across Middle-earth to the Cracks of Doom, there to destroy the Ring and foil the Dark Lord plans.

The Two Towers

Published Date:

11 November 1954


Following the events in The Fellowship of the Ring, Uruk-hai sent by Saruman and other Orcs sent by Sauron kill Boromir and capture Merry and Pippin. Aragorn, Gimli and Legolas debate which pair of hobbits to follow. Meanwhile, Frodo and Sam capture Gollum.

20 October 1954

The Adventures of Tom Bombadil

Published Date:

20 November 1962


The book contains 16 poems, two of which feature Tom Bombadil, a character encountered by Frodo Baggins in The Fellowship of the Ring. The rest of the poems are an assortment of bestiary verse and fairy tale rhyme. Three of the poems appear in The Lord of the Rings.

Smith of Wooton Mayor

Published Date:

9 November 1967


The village of Wootton Major was well known for its annual festivals. The biggest festival of all was the Feast of Good Children. This festival was celebrated only once every twenty-four years: twenty-four children of the village were invited to a party, and the highlight of the party was the Great Cake.

Timeline

1892

In 3 January 1892, John Ronald Reuel Tolkien was born in Bloemfontein, South Africa.

1896

Tolkien's father died and he, his mother and his younger brother Hilary returned to England and settled in the west Midlands.

Tolkien joined King Edward's School for boys, where he founded the TCBS (Tea Club, Barrovian Society, named after their meeting place at the Barrow Stores) with a group of fellow students; they made up new languages for fun.

1900

The family was received into the Roman Catholic church.

1904

Following the death of their mother, the two brothers were sent to live with their aunt and then in a succession of boarding houses. Their spiritual welfare was looked after by a Catholic priest, Father Francis Morgan.

1911

Tolkien went to Exeter College, Oxford to study classics, Old English and Germanic languages, receiving a second-class degree which he later increased to a first.

1914

Tolkien enlisted as a second lieutenant in the Lancashire Fusiliers, and continued to write in his spare time.

1916

Married Edith Bratt in Warwick. He fought in the Battle of the Somme, but caught trench fever and was shipped back to England, spending a month in a hospital in Birmingham.

1918

Appointed assistant lexicographer on the New English Dictionary, soon to become the Oxford English Dictionary.

1920

Became reader in English Language at the University of Leeds.

1922

Published A Middle English Vocabulary, which made his name as a scholar in the field.

1925

Published a translation of the medieval poem, Sir Gawain and The Green Knight, with fellow academic EV Gordon, and became Professor of Anglo-Saxon at Oxford University.

1930

Tolkien created an informal writing group, called The Inklings, with a group of friends in Oxford; members included CS Lewis, Owen Barfield and Charles Williams. They met every Tuesday morning at an Oxford pub, The Eagle and Child, for conversation, drinking, and readings from their work-in-progress.

One day, when he was marking examination papers, Tolkien discovered that a candidate had left one page of an answer-book blank. On the blank page he wrote: "In a hole in the ground there lived a Hobbit."

1936

A publishing house employee caught a glimpse of the incomplete typescript of The Hobbit and urged Tolkien to finish it. In this year he also gave his landmark lecture, The Monsters and the Critics, which was a trenchant look at the critical tradition on Beowulf. He argued that scholars had become too focused on criticising the poem's historical inaccuracies rather than celebrating the beauty of its language.

1937

The first edition of The Hobbit was published to great enthusiasm. It received several awards, including the prestigious New York Herald Tribune prize as the year's best children's book.

1938

After the huge success of The Hobbit, Tolkien's publishers were keen for a follow-up. Tolkien submitted The Silmarillion, his epic tale of the creation and histories of the characters that were to appear in The Lord of the Rings. However, his publisher George Unwin believed it was were "commercially unpublishable", and it remained so until 1977.

1949

Tolkien published Farmer Giles Of Ham, an Arthurian fairy tale.

1954

The first part of The Lord of the Rings trilogy, The Fellowship of the Ring, was published to mixed reviews. Leading critic Edmund Wilson dismissed it as "balderdash", but the poet WH Auden compared it to Paradise Lost and wrote: "For anyone who likes the genre to which it belongs, the Heroic Quest, I cannot imagine a more wonderful Christmas present... No fiction I have read in the last five years has given me more joy than The Fellowship of the Ring."

Fellow Inkling CS Lewis also gave it a rave review. "If Ariosto rivalled it in invention (in fact he did not) he would still lack its heroic seriousness. No imaginary world has been projected which is at once multifarious and so true to its own inner laws; none so seemingly objective, so disinfected from the taint of an author's merely individual psychology; none so relevant to the actual human situation yet free from allegory. And what fine shading there is in the variations of style to meet the endless diversity of scenes and characters - comic, homely, epic, monstrous, or diabolic."

1955

The second and third parts of The Lord of The Rings were published: The Two Towers and The Return of the King.

1959

Tolkien retired from his professorship.

1962

A poetry collection, The Adventures of Tom Bombadil, published.

1964

A collection of essays and poems, Tree and Leaf, printed.

1965

Publicity over an unauthorised paperback version of Lord of the Rings attracted readers in America, who adopted it as the bible of the "alternative society".

1967

Published a fairy tale, Smith Of Wootton Major.

1971

Edith died, and Tolkien returned to Oxford.

1973

Tolkien died in 2 September 1973.

1977

The Silmarillion was finally published, edited by Tolkien's son Christopher.

1995

Roverandom, a story Tolkien wrote for his family about a dog who is turned into a toy by a wizard, was published with the author's own illustrations.

1997

The Lord of the Rings came top in four separate polls of books of the century; The Hobbit was never far behind.
